Research

My doctoral research focuses on protest participation. More specifically, I examine the micro-level predictors and outcomes of interpersonal protest recruiting (aka. "asking others" to protest). I mainly use quantitative panel survey data to analyze my research questions. Thematically, I have covered labor union demonstrations, solidarity movements, and the climate movement.

Together with valued coauthors and excellent promotors, I have published two international descriptive reports on Fridays For Future and one peer-reviewed essay on contemporary climate activism (combined 860+ citations); four other peer-reviewed articles in top-ranking sociology journals (on the topic of interpersonal protest recruiting); and three public reports (on Belgian protests). Two other peer-reviewed publications and one encyclopedia entry are currently under review or forthcoming. Please find my publications here.

I am determined to grow as an empirical social scientist. Topics related to agonistics (C. Mouffe), affective polarization, and populism (m.n. conspiracy beliefs) are dear to me. Regarding protest, I believe the routinization/banalization of protest vs. violence deserves empirical attention.

Data skills

Using a predominantly quantitative methodological approach, I have gained real experience with data handling and (advanced) analysis (e.g. longitudinal egocentric network data). I consider myself an expert with Qualtrics and Stata, and an ongoing student of Python and R (for data viz). I currently assist academic professionals with various aspects of survey programming, dataset merging and preparation, as well as designing data visualizations.

I have co-taught classes on multiple regression and scale construction to bachelor students. I keep Feynman in mind when teaching others or myself new material.

I take pride in having a solid understanding of the basics in statistics. Please, do ask me about the Central Limit Theorem, degrees of freedom, or what the p-value literally means.
